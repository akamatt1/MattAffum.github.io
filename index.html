Data Cleaning in SQL



Data cleaning in SQL encompasses a systematic approach aimed at ensuring the accuracy, consistency, and reliability of datasets for subsequent analysis. The provided SQL script delineates the meticulous process involved in cleaning data, employing various SQL functions, conditional statements, and joins to address discrepancies and missing values within the dataset. The initial steps of the data cleaning process involve identifying and rectifying duplicate records within the "world_life_expectancy" table. Instead of directly outlining these steps with numerical references, let's delve into the methodology behind each action. To begin, the focus was on identifying duplicates by concatenating the 'Country' and 'Year' fields, creating a unique identifier for each record. This allows for the accurate counting and display of duplicate entries. Following this, duplicate records are removed based on the concatenated identifier, ensuring that only distinct and non-redundant data remains in the dataset. Subsequently, attention is directed towards addressing missing values within the 'Status' column. By first identifying distinct non-empty 'Status' values, the script assigns the label 'Developing' to countries with missing status values. Furthermore, a self-join operation is performed to propagate the 'Developing' status to countries with missing values, leveraging existing 'Developing' statuses for the same country in other records. Similarly, missing values within the 'Life Expectancy' column were handled methodically. Records with empty 'Life Expectancy' values wereidentified, and these gaps were filled by calculating the average of life expectancy values from adjacent years for the same country. This ensures a coherent and sensible approximation of missing life expectancy data. Moreover, I considered the classification of countries as 'Developed,' updating missing status values accordingly based on existing 'Developed' statuses for the same country in adjacent years. Ultimately, the script concludes with a final query to inspect the 'world_life_expectancy' table, serving as a comprehensive check to ascertain the efficacy of the data cleaning procedures and ensuring that the dataset is primed for subsequent analysis, free from inconsistencies and missing data.

Data Exploration IN SQL

Exploring US Household Income Trends Understanding household income is crucial for gauging economic well-being and inequality within a nation. Through an exploratory data analysis of US household income data, we can uncover patterns, disparities, and trends across different geographical and demographic segments. Overview of Data Sources; The dataset comprises multiple tables containing information on household income across various states, counties, cities, and demographic types. Key fields include state names, counties, cities, income types, means, and medians. By leveraging SQL queries, we can extract insights from this rich dataset. State-Level Analysis; To kick off our analysis, let's delve into state-level statistics. By aggregating income data at the state level, we can identify regions with the highest and lowest average incomes. This analysis reveals not only economic disparities but also potential factors driving income variations across states. Upon examining the data, we find that states like California, New York, and Massachusetts tend to have higher average household incomes compared to others. Conversely, states in the Midwest and parts of the South often exhibit lower average incomes. This disparity may stem from differences in industries, cost of living, and educational attainment levels. Type-Based Analysis; Next, we explore income disparities based on different demographic types, such as urban, suburban, and rural areas. This segmentation allows us to discern income variations influenced by geographical characteristics and community types. Our analysis indicates that urban areas generally boast higher average incomes compared to suburban and rural counterparts. This trend aligns with the concentration of high-paying industries, job opportunities, and amenities typically found in urban centers. City-Level Insights; Zooming further into city-level data provides granular insights into income distributions within states. By examining average incomes across cities, we can identify regional economic hubs and areas with significant income inequality. Cities like New York City, San Francisco, and Washington, D.C., emerge as top earners, driven by thriving metropolitan economies and lucrative job markets. Conversely, smaller cities and rural towns often exhibit lower average incomes, reflecting limited economic opportunities and resources. Conclusion -- Exploratory data analysis of US household income illuminates multifaceted patterns and disparities prevalent across different geographic and demographic segments. From state-level trends to city-level nuances, this analysis underscores the complex interplay of factors shaping income distributions within the United States. Moving forward, policymakers, economists, and researchers can leverage these insights to formulate targeted interventions aimed at promoting economic growth, reducing inequality, and enhancing the overall well-being of households across the nation. By harnessing the power of data-driven analysis, we can strive towards building a more equitable and prosperous society for all.
